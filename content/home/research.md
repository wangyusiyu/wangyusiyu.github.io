### Investor Political Beliefs and Firm Voluntary Disclosures: Evidence from the Umbrella Movement *(Job market paper)*

```
<div style="text-align: justify">  

**Abstract:** The Umbrella Movement, a series of street protests, occurred in Hong Kong in 2014. Protesters aimed to put pressure on the Chinese central government to apply universal suffrage in electing the Hong Kong Chief Executive in 2017. Using a group of Chinese firms cross-listed on the Hong Kong stock exchange, I find that investors form divergent beliefs around the Umbrella Movement about firms that are politically connected to the Chinese central government. This finding suggests that the weight of investors’ political beliefs in their investment decisions is subject to exogenous political events. I further document that government-connected firms increase their voluntary disclosures in general as a response to the divergent beliefs of investors. These results highlight the importance of investor beliefs in changing the information environment of firms.

</div>

### Auditors in Unhappy Cities: Do Spatial Differences Have an Impact on Audit Going Concern Opinions?

**Abstract:** I investigate whether the local happiness in the geographical area in which audit offices are located influences auditors’ going concern opinions. I find that non-Big four audit offices located in the happier U.S. Metropolitan Statistical Areas (MSAs) are less likely to issue going concern opinions. I also find that the effects of local happiness on auditors’ going concern opinions are stronger when the auditors and their clients are in the same city, or the driving distances between them are within 100 kilometers. Additional tests provide support to the argument that auditors in the happy MSAs have additional private information to mitigate their concerns when assessing the going concern status of their clients.
